Nocturnal urban heat island intensity and heat-related mortality: a multi-city panel

A. Nowak, M. Okafor, L. Reyes
Institute for Urban Climate & Health · corresponding: a.nowak@example.org
AbstractWe assemble a twelve-city panel linking hourly urban heat island (UHI) intensity to daily all-cause mortality among adults over 65. Using a distributed-lag non-linear model, we find that nocturnal UHI intensity above roughly four degrees is associated with a steep, non-linear rise in mortality odds, independent of daytime maxima. The effect concentrates in dense, low-albedo districts and persists after adjusting for background climate.
1 · Introduction

Heat-related mortality is driven less by peak afternoon temperature than by the failure of cities to cool overnight. When the urban fabric re-radiates stored daytime heat, residents are denied the nocturnal recovery period that regulates cardiovascular strain, and the physiological burden accumulates across consecutive warm nights.

Prior work has quantified UHI magnitude and, separately, the temperature–mortality curve. Fewer studies isolate the nocturnal component at the district scale, where exposure is most heterogeneous. This paper links the two using a panel design that holds slow-moving city-level confounders fixed and exploits within-city night-to-night variation.

The non-linearity in Figure 1 motivates a threshold specification rather than a linear exposure term: below about four degrees the association is weak, above it the slope steepens sharply, consistent with the exhaustion of overnight thermoregulation.

Section 2 describes the panel and the distributed-lag model; Section 3 reports the exposure–response surface and district-level heterogeneity; Section 4 discusses adaptation implications for low-albedo neighbourhoods.

2 · Data and methods

We drew hourly air temperature from paired reference and district stations across twelve mid-latitude cities over 2003–2019. Nocturnal UHI intensity was computed as the district-minus-rural difference averaged over the 00:00–05:00 window, then matched to daily mortality counts for adults over 65.

Exposure was entered through a distributed-lag non-linear model with lags to 21 days, adjusting for season, day-of-week, relative humidity, and a slow trend. City fixed effects absorb population structure and healthcare capacity.

3 · Results

Each additional degree of nocturnal UHI above the four-degree threshold corresponded to a six to nine percent rise in mortality odds, with the steepest response in basin cities. Daytime maxima added little once the nocturnal term was included.

District-level analysis localised the burden to dense, low-albedo cores, where the overnight difference from the urban edge exceeded five degrees on more than forty nights per summer.
